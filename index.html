Revolut

won Spain, but on an infrastructure that can't serve it
The biggest missteps
01
Scale became the thing regulators wouldn't let through

Revolut's operating thesis was to get to irreversible customer scale faster than anyone could catch them. It worked: 6M users in Spain, 10M in the UK before regulators could verify the core. But at that scale, automated AML triggers lock thousands of customers out every month with no human escalation and no Spanish phone line. The very thing that won them the market is now the thing they can't fix without breaking their unit economics. The service gap has become a licensing blocker in their home market, and the Spanish support rating sits at 2.9/5.

Sources

02
Massive Spanish spend, no Spanish primary-bank product

1,500 employees in Spain. 50 Barcelona ATMs, the first in the world. And still no mortgages, no BNPL, no Social Security processing. Revolut built local presence without building the local product. BNPL launched in Ireland and Poland but not Spain. Mortgages are "announced for 2026-2027" with no regulatory pathway — the Spanish lending roadmap is vaporware. The result: Spanish users stay on Revolut as a secondary account while their primary bank remains elsewhere. The brand says primary; the product says second wallet.

Slide 2 / 6 — Bunq

Bunq

seven years in Spain, still trapped in the expat niche
The biggest missteps
01
Won the wedge, never built the conversion path

Bunq's no-NIE onboarding for expats was surgical — genuinely the best in Europe. But winning a beachhead is not winning a market. Seven years into Spain, Bunq still doesn't have Bizum in the free tier, no SEPE, no AEAT, no TGSS, no Spanish-language human support. They won the expat who lands in Madrid without an NIE and never built what that user needs to stay when they become resident and want a primary bank. Bizum finally launched in May 2025 — four years late, and only in paid tiers that cost €3.99 to €18.99 per month in a market where 28M Spaniards get it free by default. Culturally dead on arrival for native users.

Sources
"Bunq integrates Bizum" — 4 years after Spain launch · The Paypers · May 2025 ↗ "Bunq lanza Bizum para sus usuarios en España" — paid tiers only · Infobae ↗
02
Founder-absolute governance, no external discipline

Niknam self-funded ~€98.5M and retains >85% control. That gave Bunq patience, profitability and boldness most VC neobanks never had. It also gave them no external discipline: three tier rebrands, a Finn chatbot their own founder admits is "still not near where we would like it to be", an EasyGroup trademark lawsuit they lost publicly, a US banking charter withdrawn after 301 days, and a support function that delivers 4-month frozen accounts with no human recourse. Profitability itself is an ECB rate bet — €85M net profit is interest on €8B+ in deposits. 100bps of cuts wipes it out.

Qonto

€100M in Barcelona, no complete product to sell
The biggest missteps
01
Local presence without local product

Qonto opened a Barcelona support hub with €100M in investment — one of the biggest B2B fintech bets on Spain in the last five years. But they launched into Spain without TGSS collaborator status (can't process Social Security payments), without Bizum (missing >95% of Spain's P2P market), and without a free plan in a market where free business banking is standard. Every Spanish autónomo using Qonto still needs a parallel Spanish bank account. That parallel account keeps the door permanently open for every competitor. Qonto has never publicly disclosed its Spanish user count — in its own internal research the company labels Spain "limited expansion" compared to France and Germany.

Sources
"Qonto opens Barcelona hub with €100M investment" · Maddyness ↗ Qonto discloses 600K+ across 8 markets — Spain never broken out ↗
02
Premium subscription in Europe's most price-sensitive banking market

Qonto's cheapest plan in Spain is €29 per month. They are the only competitor charging from day one. Meanwhile BBVA, Santander and Sabadell now offer free business accounts with up to €720 in cash incentives — the Spanish freelancer does the math in five seconds. Qonto can't match without destroying the subscription economics that make the business profitable in France and Germany. It's a pricing squeeze with no exit. The model that works in Paris and Berlin structurally cannot translate to Madrid, and Qonto can't change it without rewriting the company.

Slide 4 / 6 — N26
Missteps in Spain
monzo

N26

had every Spanish advantage and still lost to Revolut
The biggest missteps
01
The 2020 SEPE scandal permanently templated "neobank isn't a real bank"

In 2020, during COVID, Spanish users who had closed their BBVA accounts to move to N26 discovered they could not receive SEPE unemployment benefits because N26 was not a collaborating entity with Seguridad Social. The story ran in elDiario.es, Cinco Días and El Independiente for weeks. "Neobank isn't a real bank" became the default Spanish press frame for anyone pitching a digital challenger — and N26 inherits it to this day. N26 only became a SEPE collaborator in September 2023, more than three years after the scandal. The gap between having the rails and having the trust turned out to be three years long, and the damage has never been fully undone.

Sources
"N26 leaves Spanish clients without unemployment benefits" · Spain's News 2020 ↗ "N26, criticado por estafadores y clientes sin prestación en el paro" · elDiario.es ↗
02
Rails without reach, and a founder who never had the regulator conversation

N26 was the first neobank with a Spanish IBAN (2019), the first with Bizum, and the only one with Social Security integration. Every structural advantage. And Revolut arrived years later with none of those rails and still captured 6x the Spanish users. Why? In Stalf's own words to Sifted: "We had a banking licence for five years before having any discussion with the regulator." The BaFin growth cap in 2021 then froze N26 at 50K new customers per month for three years, exactly the window when Revolut scaled freely. Both co-founders were forced out inside four months in 2025. Product readiness is not market presence — N26 proved that the hard way.

Slide 5 / 6 — WiZink
Missteps in Spain
monzo

WiZink

one Spanish Supreme Court ruling destroyed the core
The biggest missteps
01
Built the whole business on APRs the Supreme Court made retroactively illegal

Värde Partners bought WiZink in 2014 as a pure PE play on Spanish revolving credit cards at 20-27% APR. It worked for five years. Then in March 2020 the Spanish Supreme Court issued STS 149/2020: reinterpreting the 1908 Ley Azcárate, it ruled that a 26.82% APR card inherited from Citibank was usurious. Contracts voided. Excess interest refundable on the entire legacy book. STS 258/2023 then fixed the quantitative rule: any revolving APR above the Bank of Spain average + 6 percentage points = usurious. WiZink did not see the ruling coming, had not stress-tested their pricing model against it, and could not pivot the unit economics in time.

Sources
STS 149/2020 — "Usura en tarjetas revolving" · Legal Today ↗ STS 258/2023 fixes the rule: BdE average + 6pp = usurious · Abogacía Española ↗
02
PE roll-up with no Spanish brand to defend them

WiZink was not built as a bank with customers. It was assembled as a portfolio — Banco Popular's consumer credit unit, plus Citibank Spain's cards, plus Barclaycard Spain's cards — acquired through airport stands, mall kiosks and co-brand deals. 1.6M Spanish customers, zero brand equity. When STS 149/2020 hit, nobody defended them. Arriaga Asociados, Reclamador, Indemniza and a dozen other firms industrialised "reclamar WiZink" into a household phrase. €81M in revolving provisions in 2024 alone. Losses widening again in 2025. The 2025-2029 plan targets profitability only in 2027. Three CEOs through the crisis. The PE yield-extraction model had no cultural moat to absorb the legal shock.

Slide 6 / 6 — Trade Republic
Missteps in Spain
monzo

Trade Republic

1M Spanish users, but none of them see it as a bank
The biggest missteps
01
Ran Spain on a German central stack for four years

Trade Republic launched in Spain in October 2021 and did not ship a Spanish IBAN, Bizum or a local branch until June 2025 — four years later. For those four years, every Spanish user was operating on a German stack with German-first support, German tax documentation, and no local ops team. The result, in Phasen's own Spanish research: primary-bank NPS of 10-20 versus 35-45 for investing. Users explicitly say "bueno para tener cash, pero no lo uses como banco principal." The CNMV had to publicly warn about phishing clones of Trade Republic — which flourished precisely because Spanish users could not reach real support. TR now has 1M Spanish users who trust it as a broker and do not trust it as a bank.

Sources
"Trade Republic Opens National Branches in France, Spain, Italy" · Jan 2025 ↗ CNMV alerts: phishing clone of Trade Republic · Territorio Fintech ↗ "Bueno para cash, no como banco principal" · Rankia forum ↗
02
Built the entire business on a revenue stream the EU is about to ban

Over a third of Trade Republic's revenue comes from Payment for Order Flow. The EU ban on PFOF hits on 30 June 2026. The MTF licence they obtained in January 2026 is a mitigation bet, not a proven replacement. The moment PFOF ends, TR either raises commissions — breaking the "€1 trades" promise that acquired 10M users — or absorbs a 30%+ revenue hit. Meanwhile the users themselves came for the deal, not the brand. Revolut, N26 and Wise are all matching the offer. Spanish users in particular came for €1 trades and 2% TAE, and they will leave in five minutes the moment someone beats the price. Mercenary customers with zero switching cost.
